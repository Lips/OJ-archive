Some of you maybe know I've been working for a record factory, Brooklynphono, for a little while. I thought maybe I'd post the pricing in case anyone was interested in making some awesome (and relatively cheap I think) records!

Here's the website: http://www.brooklynphono.com/

Pricing for 12" LPs:

Mastering per side (side under 20 minutes) - $165.00
Mastering per side (side over 20 minutes) - $200.00
Plating per side (1 step and replacements) - $85.00
Plating per side (2 step) - $135.00

Labels printing:
Black and White - $150.00
Full Color - $175.00

Sleeves - $0.00
Jackets (stock black or white, with or without hole) - $0.35

Press set up charges - $150.00

Test pressing (5) - $25.00

140 gram record (black vinyl) - $1.25
(color vinyl is also available - ask for pricing)

Shrink-wrap (per record) - $0.15

Stickering and inserting (per item/per record) - $0.05

Things that are optional: Printed labels, Jackets , Shrink-wrap, and Stickers
Also there are no minimums!

What I know

Actually, the Joe Crow project I'm working on, I'm doing with another firm, just because it will be a 10-inch... but I have Brooklyn Phono earmarked for what's next.

There are 2 types of mastering you need to do.

One is the mastering of the digital file, so that the EQ is good, etc. etc.

Second is the lacquer mastering, which is the actual creation of the grooves based on your digital file. From what I understand, they don't do any "correction" to the sound at this point, unless your file is creating unstable grooves that will skip.

When I did the TPM 7-inch, I just went to Paul Gold at Salt Mastering and he both did a digital master and cut the lacquers. Then he sent the lacquers off to the pressing plant and they made their metal parts from that.

The plates are metal stampers made from the lacquers. I'm not 100% on this, but I think one-step is that they make a "negative" piece of metal from the lacquer and then use it to stamp out your records. 2-Step is they make a "positive" from the negative, in order to make new replacement stampers when they wear out. I think you have to do more than 500 copies for the stamper to start to wear out, so 2-step probably isn't necessary if you're not Arcade Fire. (I assume.)

When you just give a digital file to a pressing plant, I do not fully understand the limitations of frequency that need to be taken into account with the file you give them, since vinyl "understands" less of the dynamic range than digital. I know that you don't want too much bass unless you're doing like a 12-inch at 45rpm, or something.

That's about as much as I know.

couple answers to some really good questions

I emailed these to one of the smart guys I work with but he's really busy and never got around to answering them, so I'll try to the best of my ability.

Justin is pretty much right on about all of that stuff, except that we get easily 1500 records out of a single set of stampers. Mastering just means cutting the lacquers, I found this pretty nifty article on what that entails:
http://www.recordingmag.com/resources/resourceDetail/114.html

Unfortunately there's no worksheet, you kind of have to call and get a quote. The good thing about that is that Paul, the guy who takes the orders, is really awesome and has definitely been known to give discounts. He offered to give me a cut of any jobs that I "brokered" but I said he should just give you guys a discount instead, so I think that's quite feasible.

As for glitter, I'm pretty sure it would interfere with the sound, but I asked one of the guys at work and he said what you could probably do is make a picture disc with glittery paper instead of a picture. Unfortunately we can't do those :( For leaves and collages, I know that any paper that gets included on a record, including the labels, has to go into an oven to take out any moisture which will damage the vinyl. At our place their set at around 125 celsius. A lot of inks melt, some burn, and I bet most adhesives would melt too, but maybe you could find one meant for high heat. Leaves, I'm not sure maybe they'd burn or the heat would destroy the colors, but maybe not! I also bet that the sheets of paper get sent through a machine, I'm not sure whether a collage would make it through or not, but you could try calling these guys, seems like they're up for anything: http://www.furnacemfg.com/

And finally, some GOOD NEWS!
-We're going to have a winter special on LP's, they haven't posted the rates yet, but I'll repost them here as soon as they're up on the facebook page (Hey, anyone feel like "liking" Brooklynphono? http://www.facebook.com/pages/Brooklynphono/133931046660115)
-Also, we're currently pressing a few jobs in white and we'll probably have some leftover, AND I just discovered a stash of a bunch of extra colored material - green, red, blue, yellow, etc. I asked and it's official, this means that cute rainbow sprinkle on vanilla ice cream colored records are totally possible, at least for a little while! I'm not sure exactly what it will look like, but I'm pretty sure awesome is part of it.
